# Solution Space · Test

> Goal: put your prototype in front of real people, learn what's wrong, and iterate. Testing closes the solution diamond, and it's where you find out whether your idea survives contact with reality.

Testing is not validation-seeking. If you go in hoping to be proven right, you'll unconsciously steer users toward praise and learn nothing. Go in hoping to be proven *wrong* quickly and cheaply, that's how testing pays off.

> 🧪 You're testing the idea, not defending it. Disconfirming feedback is the valuable kind.

---

## Tools & methods in this phase

- **User testing** — watch real users interact with the prototype; observe behavior, don't just ask opinions.
- **Feedback** — capture reactions, confusion, delight, and friction, in the user's own words.
- **Iteration** — feed learnings straight back into the prototype (or back to earlier phases).
- **Business Model Canvas** — map how the whole solution creates, delivers, and captures value.
- **Lean Canvas** — a startup-focused one-page business model (problem, solution, metrics, unfair advantage).
- **Value Proposition Canvas** — check the fit between your solution and the user's jobs/pains/gains.

---

## Where AI genuinely helps

**1. Synthesizing test feedback into themes**
Turn many test sessions into patterns of confusion, delight, and friction, traced to specific moments.

**2. Drafting the canvases**
AI can populate first-draft Business Model, Lean, and Value Proposition canvases from your inputs, which you challenge and correct.

**3. Spotting what you're avoiding**
Ask AI to find the negative signals you might be downplaying because you like the idea.

**4. Suggesting iteration directions**
Based on feedback themes, AI can propose what to change, which you prioritize.

---

## Where AI will mislead you

- **AI can help you rationalize.** If you feed it feedback and ask "is this good?", it can find a positive spin. Ask it instead to surface the disconfirming signal.
- It wasn't in the room. It misses tone, hesitation, body language, the texture that tells you what a user really felt.
- Canvas drafts can look authoritative while resting on untested assumptions. A filled-in Lean Canvas is a set of hypotheses, not facts.
- It can average away the one outlier reaction that's actually the most important signal.

---

## Ready-to-use prompts

**Prompt: Synthesize test feedback (find the disconfirming signal)**
```
Here are my user test notes: [paste]. Identify the main themes — confusion,
friction, delight — and tie each to the specific moment it occurred. Then
specifically surface the DISCONFIRMING signals: where did the prototype fail or
users struggle? Don't soften it. Flag anything that challenges my core assumption.
```

**Prompt: Draft a Value Proposition Canvas fit-check**
```
User's jobs/pains/gains: [paste]. Our solution: [describe]. Map our
pain-relievers and gain-creators against their actual pains and gains. Where is
the fit STRONG, and where are we claiming a fit the evidence doesn't support?
```

**Prompt: Draft a Lean Canvas**
```
Based on what we know: [paste context], draft a Lean Canvas (problem, customer
segments, unique value proposition, solution, channels, revenue, cost, key
metrics, unfair advantage). Mark each box as "evidenced" or "assumption" so I
can see what still needs testing.
```

**Prompt: Prioritize iterations**
```
Here are the feedback themes: [paste]. Propose iteration options, and help me
prioritize by impact on the core user problem vs. effort. Flag which changes
address real evidence vs. one-off opinions.
```

---

## Before moving to Pitch (or looping back), check:

- [ ] Did I watch behavior, or just collect polite opinions?
- [ ] Did I actively look for disconfirming signals, not just validation?
- [ ] Did I capture the outlier reaction, not average it away?
- [ ] Are my canvas boxes marked as evidenced vs. assumption?
- [ ] Do I know what to iterate, and whether to loop back to an earlier phase?
- [ ] Is the solution's fit to real jobs/pains/gains proven, or still hoped for?

> 🔁 Design thinking is a loop, not a line. Testing often sends you back to Ideate, Point of View, or even Observe. That's success, not failure.
