# Problem Space · Point of View

> Goal: converge. Take everything from Understand and Observe and distill it into ONE sharp, worth-solving problem statement. This is the phase that closes the problem diamond, and the hinge of the entire process.

Everything before this diverged, gathering, exploring, widening. Point of View narrows. Get it right and the solution space has a clear target. Get it wrong and you'll build a great answer to the wrong question.

> ⚠️ Last checkpoint before solutions. The problem statement you leave here with is the brief for everything that follows.

---

## Tools & methods in this phase

- **Generate insights** — turn raw findings into meaningful "aha" observations about the user.
- **Create personas** — a grounded, evidence-based archetype of who you're designing for.
- **Customer profile** — the user's jobs, pains & gains, structured (Value Proposition Canvas language).
- **Insights statements** — crisp statements capturing a surprising, actionable truth.
- **Summarize all information to a clear point of view** — one coherent stance on the problem.
- **Problem statement** — a sharp articulation of the problem, who has it, and why it matters.
- **How Might We (HMW)** — reframe the problem as open opportunities: "How might we...?"

---

## Where AI genuinely helps

**1. Clustering findings into insights**
AI can group scattered observations and propose candidate insights, which you sharpen and validate.

**2. Drafting evidence-based personas**
Feed it your verified jobs/pains/gains and get a persona draft, then strip out anything not backed by data.

**3. Generating problem-statement and HMW variations**
AI is fast at reframing one problem ten different ways, some open directions you hadn't considered.

**4. Stress-testing for hidden assumptions**
The highest-value use: ask AI to expose the assumptions your problem statement smuggles in.

---

## Where AI will mislead you

- AI gravitates toward **generic, safe problem statements.** The sharp, specific, slightly uncomfortable framing, the one that actually reflects your users, comes from you.
- **Personas are where fabrication creeps in.** AI will happily add plausible demographic detail that no research supports. Keep personas ruthlessly evidence-based.
- A confident sentence is not a validated one. AI can make a thin insight *sound* rigorous.
- It doesn't know which problem is worth solving for your mission or business. That prioritization is a human, value-laden call.

## Ready-to-use prompts

**Prompt: Cluster findings into insights**
```
Here are my verified jobs, pains & gains with supporting quotes: [paste].
Cluster them into 3-5 candidate INSIGHTS — surprising, actionable truths about
the user. For each, cite the evidence it rests on. Flag any insight that's
thin or under-supported.
```

**Prompt: Draft an evidence-based persona**
```
Using ONLY these verified findings: [paste jobs/pains/gains + quotes], draft a
persona. Include only attributes the evidence supports. For anything you're
tempted to add for realism (age, income, etc.), list it separately as
"unsupported — verify or cut." Do not invent detail.
```

**Prompt: Draft candidate problem statements**
```
Based on these insights: [paste], draft 5 candidate problem statements:
"[Specific user] struggles to [do something] because [insight], which matters
because [consequence]." Keep each at the level of a PROBLEM, not a solution.
Flag any that smuggle in a solution.
```

**Prompt: Generate How Might We questions**
```
Take this problem statement: [statement]. Generate 10 "How might we..."
reframings that open different solution directions — some broad, some narrow,
some that challenge the framing itself.
```

**Prompt: Expose my assumptions**
```
Here's my problem statement: [statement]. List every assumption it makes about
the user, context, and cause. For each, rate the risk (what happens if it's
wrong) and name the evidence that would confirm or kill it.
```

---

## Before entering the Solution Space, check:

- [ ] Is this a problem statement, or a solution in disguise?
- [ ] Can I trace it to real research evidence, not just a plausible sentence?
- [ ] Is my persona free of invented detail, everything backed by data?
- [ ] Is the problem specific enough to act on, but not so narrow it presupposes the answer?
- [ ] Would my users actually recognize this as their problem?
- [ ] Is it worth solving, for them and for us?
