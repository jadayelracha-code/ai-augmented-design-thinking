# Problem Space · Understand

> Goal: understand the problem before you talk to users. Get expert on the domain, make your assumptions and hypotheses explicit, and build a research plan. **Important: no solutions yet!**

This is the quiet phase everyone skips. Understand is where you turn vague curiosity into a sharp research plan, and where you resist the powerful urge to jump to answers.

> ⚠️ **The one rule of this phase: No solutions!** The moment you start designing, you stop understanding.

---

## Tools & methods in this phase

- **Become an instant expert** — rapidly get up to speed on the domain, market, and prior art.
- **Game plan** — your approach for the problem-space work ahead.
- **Assumptions** — surface what you're currently taking for granted.
- **Hypotheses** — turn assumptions into testable statements.
- **Research plan** — who to learn from, how, and how much.
- **Interview guideline** — a prepared, non-leading interview script.
- **User journey map** — map the user's current end-to-end experience as you understand it today.

---

## Where AI genuinely helps

**1. Becoming an instant expert**
AI is excellent at rapidly briefing you on a domain, key terms, players, common patterns, so you walk into research informed. Verify claims against primary sources.

**2. Surfacing hidden assumptions**
You can't test assumptions you can't see. AI reads your brief and reflects back the assumptions baked into it.

**3. Turning assumptions into hypotheses**
Reframe "users are frustrated by X" into a falsifiable hypothesis.

**4. Drafting the interview guideline**
AI drafts open, non-leading questions and flags leading ones.

**5. First-draft user journey map**
AI can sketch a hypothesized current-state journey for you to validate later.

---

## Where AI will mislead you

- "Instant expert" briefings can contain confident errors. Cross-check anything load-bearing.
- AI will invent tidy "users" and "needs" that are pure imagination. These are *hypotheses to test*, never findings.
- It tends to propose over-ambitious research plans. Keep it lean; your real constraint is time and access.

## Ready-to-use prompts

**Prompt: Become an instant expert**
```
Brief me on [domain] as if I'm entering it for the first time: key 
concepts, main players, common user behaviors, and typical pain points. 
Flag anything you're uncertain about so I can verify it against primary sources.
```

**Prompt: Surface my assumptions**
```
Here's my project brief: [paste].
List every assumption I'm making about who the users are, what they need, 
and why the problem exists. Separate "stated" from "unstated but implied."
```

**Prompt: Convert assumptions into hypotheses**
```
Take these assumptions: [list]. Rewrite each as a testable hypothesis:
"We believe [X]. We'll know we're wrong if [observable signal]."
```

**Prompt: Draft a non-leading interview guideline**
```
I'm researching [topic] with [user type]. Draft 8-10 open-ended questions 
that surface real stories and past behavior, not opinions or hypotheticals. 
Flag any question that risks leading the interviewee.
```

**Prompt: Hypothesized user journey map**
```
Based on this context: [describe], sketch the user's current end-to-end 
journey as a series of stages with their likely actions, thoughts, and 
pain points at each. Mark this clearly as a hypothesis to validate.
```

- [ ] Have I written down my assumptions instead of pretending I have none?

- [ ] Do I have a non-leading interview guideline ready?
- [ ] Does my research plan fit my real time and access constraints?
