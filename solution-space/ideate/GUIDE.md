# Solution Space · Ideate

> Goal: find ideas, lots of them. With a sharp problem statement in hand, you now DIVERGE again, generating many possible solutions before committing to any one.

You've crossed into the solution space. The discipline flips: in the problem space you fought the urge to jump to answers. Here, answers are finally the job, but the trap is different. The trap now is converging too early, grabbing the first plausible idea instead of generating enough to find a great one.

> 💡 Quantity before quality. Judgment comes later. Right now, go wide.

---

## Tools & methods in this phase

- **Ideation workshops** — structured group sessions to generate ideas collaboratively.
- **Inspiration boards** — visual collections of stimuli, analogies, and adjacent solutions.
- **Brainstorming** — classic rapid, judgment-free idea generation.
- **Brainwriting (6-3-5 method)** — 6 people each write 3 ideas in 5 minutes, then pass and build. Structured, quiet, avoids groupthink.
- **Idea shopping** — borrow and adapt ideas from other domains and products.
- **Clustering** — group related ideas to see themes and combine them.
- **Voting** — converge democratically on the most promising ideas (dot voting, etc.).
- **Idea napkin** — capture a single idea crisply: what it is, who it's for, why it matters.

---

## Where AI genuinely helps

**1. Idea multiplication**
This is AI's superpower in design thinking. Ask for 30 ideas and get range, including angles you'd never reach alone. You then filter with human judgment.

**2. Cross-domain "idea shopping"**
AI is excellent at analogical thinking: "how do other industries solve a problem like this?" It surfaces adjacent solutions fast.

**3. Clustering at speed**
Feed it a messy list of ideas and get proposed thematic clusters to react to.

**4. Building on ideas (brainwriting partner)**
AI can act as an extra "seat" in the 6-3-5 method, extending a seed idea in directions your team then reacts to.

---

## Where AI will mislead you

- AI ideas trend toward the **generic and safe** middle. The wild, specific, breakthrough idea usually comes from a human who knows the context. Use AI for breadth, not brilliance.
- **Volume can drown judgment.** 100 AI ideas isn't progress if it buries the 3 good human ones. Curate ruthlessly.
- It doesn't feel the constraints, cost, brand, feasibility, that make an idea real or absurd for *your* situation.
- Idea shopping via AI can surface solutions that don't actually fit your users' real jobs/pains/gains. Always check ideas back against the problem statement.

## Ready-to-use prompts

**Prompt: Multiply ideas (go wide)**
```
Problem statement: [paste]. How Might We: [paste].
Generate 30 distinct solution ideas. Vary wildly: some safe, some radical, some
low-tech, some that challenge assumptions. One line each. Don't self-censor for
feasibility yet — that's the next step.
```

**Prompt: Idea shopping (cross-domain)**
```
Our users struggle with [problem]. How do OTHER industries or products solve a
structurally similar problem? Give me 10 analogies from different domains and,
for each, the transferable idea we could adapt.
```

**Prompt: Cluster ideas**
```
Here's our raw idea list: [paste]. Group them into thematic clusters. Name each
cluster and note which ideas could combine into something stronger. Flag any
duplicates.
```

**Prompt: Idea napkin**
```
Take this idea: [idea]. Write it up as an "idea napkin": what it is (1 sentence),
who it's for, the core user job it serves, why it matters, and the single biggest
risk or open question.
```

## Before moving to Prototype, check:

- [ ] Did I generate enough ideas to actually have a choice, or grab the first plausible one?
- [ ] Did I go genuinely wide, including uncomfortable or radical options?
- [ ] Did I curate with human judgment, not just keep whatever AI produced most of?
- [ ] Do my shortlisted ideas actually address the problem statement and real jobs/pains/gains?
- [ ] Have I converged (voted/clustered) down to a few worth prototyping?
